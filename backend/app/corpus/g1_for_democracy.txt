1. The State Exercises Coercive Power — and That Triggers a Reparative Duty
All states restrict liberty through laws enforced by violence. If you refuse taxes, the state threatens punishment. If you break a law, it arrests you. These restrictions are non-optional, because you never chose the state you were born into.
Mechanism: Coercion without consent creates a moral debt. When an agent restricts your freedom, they incur an obligation to compensate you.
Implication: Democracy is the mechanism through which states repay that moral debt — by allowing citizens to co-author the laws that bind them. Therefore, democracy isn’t a luxury; it’s a right flowing directly from state coercion.
2. Consent to the State Is Not Real Without Democracy
No meaningful opt-out exists.
You didn’t sign any “social contract.”
You can’t reasonably leave — immigration is expensive, socially disruptive, and most land on Earth is claimed.
Voting doesn’t indicate consent when elections force you between imperfect parties.
Mechanism: If individuals cannot refuse the coercive structure and cannot exit it, consent becomes impossible unless the state provides internal mechanisms for control.
Implication: Democracy is the only available form of consent we have. Without it, the state’s coercion is illegitimate. Therefore, democracy must be treated as a right, not a privilege.
3. Democracy Protects Freedom Better Than Any Alternative System
Two forces restrict freedom more than the state:
Violence in a lawless environment.
Poverty in an unregulated environment.
A state increases freedom by coordinating safety, providing infrastructure, preventing domination, and resolving disputes. But if the state increases freedom through law enforcement, then citizens must retain final authority over those laws — or the state becomes a source of domination again.
Mechanism: Democratic participation prevents the state from becoming the very threat it was created to neutralize.
Implication: Democracy isn’t merely instrumentally good. It is the moral precondition to freedom itself.
4. Examples (headline-level only)
Post-dictatorship South Korea: democratization massively increased personal liberties.
Tunisia’s Arab Spring moment: brief democratic opening reduced police violence and corruption.
Eastern Europe post-1990: democratic transitions restored civil freedoms stolen under authoritarianism.
